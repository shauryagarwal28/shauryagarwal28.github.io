# Portfolio Strategy

## Why This Portfolio Exists

Shaurya Agarwal is transitioning from a Business Analyst / SAP Consultant role at Signify (Philips Lighting) into Product Management. The portfolio's primary job is to close the credibility gap between "I have PM instincts" and "here is evidence of PM thinking."

The core problem this portfolio solves: most hiring managers won't take a meeting with a career-transitioning candidate without proof of structured product thinking. Teardowns, case studies, and a clean professional presence are the proof.

---

## Target Audience

**Primary:** PM hiring managers and recruiters at consumer internet, EdTech, Q-commerce, and SaaS companies in India.

**Secondary:** Nextleap alumni network, LinkedIn connections who may refer or refer-introduce.

**Tertiary:** Technical interviewers who want to verify that a CS grad can think in systems, not just build them.

---

## Portfolio Goals (in priority order)

1. **Credibility** — demonstrate structured PM thinking through teardowns and case studies.
2. **Discoverability** — live GitHub Pages URL, linkable from LinkedIn, resume, and job applications.
3. **Memorability** — distinctive visual design that doesn't look like a template.
4. **Contact conversion** — make it easy for someone to reach out after reading the work.

---

## What Success Looks Like

- A recruiter can open the portfolio, read one teardown, and understand the product thinking framework in under 5 minutes.
- A hiring manager forwarding the link to their team as "look at this candidate's teardowns."
- A PM at a target company reaching out via the contact form or email after seeing it on LinkedIn.

---

## Target Roles

Based on the profile (B.Tech CS, 2.5 years at Signify, Nextleap certified):

- Associate Product Manager (APM)
- Product Manager — 0-2 years experience
- Growth PM / Data PM roles where analytics background (SQL, Python, Power BI) is valued
- Consumer internet (EdTech, Q-commerce, fintech, SaaS)
- Preferred geography: Delhi NCR / Bangalore / remote

## What the Portfolio Does NOT Try to Do

- Showcase design skills (it's not a UX portfolio)
- Compete on visual polish alone (the teardown content is the differentiator)
- Appeal to generic tech roles — it is specifically PM-targeted
