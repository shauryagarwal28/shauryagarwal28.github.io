01 — Product Overview

What Inshorts does today

Inshorts is India's highest-rated news app, delivering news summarized in 60 words or less. It produces approximately 1,000 shorts per day, with the average user consuming 86 shorts in roughly 12 minutes daily — a remarkable engagement density.

How the current system works

Inshorts uses a hybrid editorial + AI approach. Its Rapid60 Algorithm uses AI-powered summarisation trained on 500K+ editor-written summaries, producing 100K+ summaries per month. Each story gets a rating from 0–1000; editor-rated 1000 stories are pushed to all users regardless of preferences. Lower-rated stories are matched to user interests via behavioural signals: time spent, swipe behaviour, category preferences, and implicit interest detection.

The content mix is approximately 20% editorially curated (drives highest CTR) and 80% AI-driven personalisation. This ratio is the core of the problem.

02 — Problem Analysis

Where the current system breaks down

📈

Engagement-optimised ranking amplifies sensationalism

The feed optimises for time-spent and swipe count, not information value. Sensational or clickbait content gets amplified over substantive news because it generates more engagement signals — even if users don't find it valuable.

High impact · Trust risk
🔄

No quality signal in engagement metrics

The system cannot distinguish between a user quickly swiping past a card (no interest) and genuinely reading it (interest). Low-quality stories that get swiped rapidly inflate engagement metrics just as much as good stories that get read.

High impact · Data quality
🫧

Echo chambers narrow over time

Swipe-based personalisation progressively narrows content diversity. Users who engage with tech news get more tech news until they miss important stories from other categories entirely. Pure personalisation without a diversity floor creates filter bubbles.

Medium impact · Long-term retention
🔔

Notification strategy erodes trust

Users report notifications feeling constant, non-personalised, and disruptive. Critically, notification stories are used as an advertising format — branded content as the first story — blurring editorial and commercial lines and reducing trust in all notifications.

High impact · Retention risk
"Irrelevant news keeps popping up even after dismissing as not interested." — Play Store review · 2024
03 — Proposed Solution: News Quality Score

Replacing the single ranking signal

Replace the current 0–1000 editorial score with a multi-dimensional News Quality Score (NQS) that rewards information value, not just editorial endorsement.

NQS = w₁(Source Credibility) + w₂(Editorial Rating) + w₃(Factual Density) + w₄(Uniqueness) + w₅(Timeliness)

04 — New Engagement Metrics

Measuring quality, not quantity

Current metric: screens swiped per session (volume). This must be replaced with metrics that distinguish genuine engagement from passive scrolling.

05 — Feed Composition Rules

Enforcing a quality floor

Instead of pure personalisation, enforce a feed composition that guarantees quality and diversity alongside personal relevance.

Slot%RulePurpose
Must-Know News40%Editor-curated, NQS > 800, pushed to all users (up from current 20%)Ensures every user gets high-quality important news regardless of preferences
Personalised Quality35%AI-matched to user interests, but only from stories with NQS > 500Personalisation within a quality floor — no clickbait in the personalised slot
Serendipity Slot15%High-NQS stories outside the user's usual categoriesBreaks echo chambers; exposes users to important news they wouldn't seek out
Hyperlocal10%Geo-targeted stories relevant to user's city/stateDrives local relevance; supports regional content partnerships

Fixing the negative feedback loop

The current "Not Interested" signal has weak processing. The fix: weight "Not Interested" 3x more than a passive swipe-past. Add a granular "Why not interested?" quick-select: Irrelevant topic / Already knew this / Low quality / Clickbait. This feeds directly into NQS calibration — a user reporting clickbait on a source lowers that source's credibility score.

06 — 3-Tier Notification System

From disruptive to trusted

Tier 1 — Breaking (Immediate push, max 2/day)

Criteria: NQS > 950 AND editor-flagged as breaking. Format: Rich notification with headline + 1-line summary. Delivered immediately, regardless of time. Never used for advertising. This tier must be sacred — the moment it carries a brand message, users lose trust in all breaking news alerts.

Tier 2 — Daily Digest (Scheduled, 2–3x/day)

Criteria: Top 5 stories by NQS from user's preferred categories. Format: Bundled notification ("5 stories you should know today"). Delivered at user's historically optimal engagement windows — typically morning commute (7–9 AM), lunch (12–1 PM), evening (7–9 PM). User can customise digest frequency (1x, 2x, or 3x daily).

Tier 3 — Weekly Roundup (Low-priority, 1x/week)

Criteria: Top stories the user missed from the Serendipity slot — "This week in [category you don't usually read]." Delivered Sunday morning. Goal: re-engage dormant users and expand content discovery. Category is auto-selected based on what the user engaged with least that week.

09 — PM Takeaways

What this teaches about product

Engagement metrics can actively destroy the product they're meant to serve

Optimising for swipe volume is exactly the wrong metric for an app whose value proposition is quality news in 60 words. The metric selection is itself a product decision — and Inshorts made the wrong one. Choosing metrics that align with user outcomes (dwell time, shares, return rate) rather than user activity volume (screens swiped) is the first step to fixing the product.

Notifications are a trust bank account — every bad one is a withdrawal

Blending advertising into the breaking news notification channel is a one-way door. Each branded notification reduces the trust users extend to the next breaking news alert. The 3-tier system is fundamentally about protecting the highest-trust channel (Breaking) from any monetisation pressure.

A quality floor is not the opposite of personalisation — it's the foundation for it

The NQS system doesn't eliminate personalisation; it ensures personalisation operates within a quality envelope. Users still get news they care about — they just don't get clickbait that only looks like news they'd care about.
